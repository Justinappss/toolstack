## What Is a Meta Description?

A meta description is the short snippet of text that appears beneath a page title in Google search results. It doesn't directly affect your page's ranking, but it dramatically affects click-through rate (CTR) — the percentage of people who click your result vs skipping it. A weak meta description on a #1 ranking page can lose to a #3 result with a compelling one. Google sometimes rewrites meta descriptions using your page content, but having a strong one gives you control over your messaging in search results, social shares, and link previews.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is the ideal meta description length?**

The Google SERP typically displays 150–160 characters on desktop and truncates to around 120 characters on mobile. Front-load your primary keyword and core value proposition in the first 120 characters to ensure they display on all devices. Descriptions over 160 characters aren't penalised for ranking, but they will be cut off mid-sentence with an ellipsis, which looks unprofessional and reduces CTR. This tool shows the exact truncation point in the live preview.

**Q: Does a meta description affect SEO rankings?**

Meta descriptions are not a direct ranking factor — Google has confirmed this. However, they indirectly affect SEO through click-through rate. If your result gets more clicks than Google expects for its position, that's a positive engagement signal. Conversely, a result that ranks #2 but has a weak meta description might get fewer clicks than the #4 result with a compelling one. Strong meta descriptions also reduce bounce rate because visitors arrive with accurate expectations of what the page contains.

---

**Q: Should I include keywords in the meta description?**

Yes, for two reasons. First, Google bolds keywords in the meta description that match the searcher's query, making your result visually stand out in the SERP. Second, it confirms to the searcher that your page is relevant to their search. Place your primary keyword naturally in the first sentence rather than forcing it awkwardly. Don't keyword-stuff — Google may rewrite your description if it looks spammy, and it reads poorly to humans regardless.

---

**Q: When does Google rewrite meta descriptions?**

Google rewrites meta descriptions when it determines the existing one doesn't accurately represent the page for a given query. This happens frequently — studies suggest Google rewrites meta descriptions up to 70% of the time. It's more likely to rewrite if: your description is too short or too long, it's duplicated across multiple pages, it's stuffed with keywords without readable value, or the query intent doesn't match your description. Writing high-quality, accurate descriptions reduces rewrites but doesn't eliminate them.

**Q: What makes a meta description high CTR?**

High-CTR meta descriptions typically include: the primary keyword (triggers bold highlighting in SERPs), a clear benefit or value proposition, specificity (numbers, outcomes, timeframes outperform vague claims), a subtle call to action ("See how," "Find out," "Compare"), and absence of spam triggers. Question-format descriptions work well because they mirror conversational search queries. Urgency ("limited," "before you buy") can boost CTR but should be used honestly — false urgency erodes trust.

---

**Q: Should every page have a unique meta description?**

Yes. Duplicate meta descriptions across pages tell Google your content is thin or duplicated. They also miss the opportunity to tailor the copy to the specific intent of each page. For large sites (e-commerce, directories) where writing unique descriptions for thousands of pages is impractical, use dynamic templates that pull in page-specific variables like product name, price, or category rather than leaving descriptions blank or duplicated.
