## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is expected value (EV) in card breaks?**

Expected value (EV) is the average monetary return you'd expect from opening a box if you could open it thousands of times. A positive EV means the box is theoretically worth more than it costs \u2014 you expect to profit on average. A negative EV means you expect to lose money on average. EV is a guide, not a guarantee \u2014 individual results vary widely due to luck.

**Q: How do I calculate the EV of a box break?**

EV = (hits per box for each hit type \u00d7 average value of that hit type) \u2212 box cost \u2212 platform fees \u2212 shipping. For example: a 24-pack box with 1 auto per 12 packs (2 autos expected), each worth $80, gives $160 gross. Subtract eBay fees (13.25% + $0.30 per sale) and shipping gives your net EV. **Q: What EV percentage is good for a box break?**

An EV% above 100% means positive EV \u2014 you theoretically expect to profit. Most retail and hobby boxes have an EV% of 60\u201390%, meaning collectors accept a built-in negative EV for the experience of opening. Professional breakers typically look for boxes with EV% above 85% to run viable break businesses after accounting for break fees and margin.

**Q: How do platform fees affect break EV?**

Platform fees significantly reduce EV. eBay charges 13.25% + $0.30 per card sold (Trading Cards category). Whatnot charges 10.9% + $0.30. On a $100 auto, eBay takes $13.55 and Whatnot takes $11.20 \u2014 a difference of $2.35. Across many sales this adds up. Selling locally eliminates fees entirely but limits your buyer pool and typically achieves lower prices.

**Q: Is pack EV the same as box EV?**

Box EV is the total expected value of opening an entire box. Pack EV is simply the box EV divided by the number of packs. Pack EV is useful for comparing products \u2014 if a 24-pack hobby box has an EV of $120 vs $80 box cost, the EV per pack is $5 vs $3.33 cost per pack. Most buying decisions are made at the box level, but pack EV helps compare across different box sizes.

---

**Q: How does Pok\u00e9mon box EV compare to sports cards?**

Pok\u00e9mon boxes typically have lower EV% than premium sports card hobby boxes \u2014 most modern Pok\u00e9mon sets hover around 60\u201380% EV, while sports card hobby boxes can range from 70% to over 100% for sought-after products. However, Pok\u00e9mon boxes benefit from massive secondary market liquidity \u2014 almost any hit sells quickly. Sports card EV is more player-dependent and can swing wildly based on current news.
