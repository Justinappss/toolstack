## Why Subject Lines Matter

Subject line is the single biggest lever for email open rate. Industry average open rates across all sectors hover around 21–25%. High-performing subject lines can push this to 40–60% for engaged lists. A subject line that triggers a spam filter bypasses the open rate question entirely — it never reaches the inbox. Even small improvements in subject line quality compound significantly across a list: on a 10,000-subscriber list, moving from 20% to 30% open rate means 1,000 additional people reading your email every send.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is a good email subject line score?**

A score of 80 or above (grade A) is excellent — your subject line has strong power words, appropriate length, no spam triggers, and clear value signalling. A score of 65–79 (grade B) is good and will perform well. Aim for at least 65 before sending. Scores below 50 typically indicate either a spam trigger issue or a subject line that's too vague to compel opens. The score is a guide, not a guarantee — list quality, sending reputation, and timing all affect real-world open rates.

**Q: What is the ideal email subject line length?**

40–60 characters is the sweet spot for desktop email clients. Mobile Gmail and Apple Mail typically display 30–40 characters before truncating, so your hook should be front-loaded in the first 35 characters for maximum mobile impact. Subject lines under 20 characters often feel too vague. Subject lines over 70 characters are clipped on most clients and lose impact. In terms of words: 5–9 words tends to perform best across studies. Numbers in subject lines (e.g., "7 ways to...") consistently outperform non-numeric equivalents.

---

**Q: What are spam trigger words in email subject lines?**

Spam triggers are words and phrases that email filters — and suspicious human readers — associate with unsolicited or deceptive mail. Common triggers include: "Free" (especially in all caps), "Congratulations", "You've been selected", "Act now", "Limited time offer", "Guaranteed", "No risk", "100% free", "Make money", "Winner", and excessive punctuation like "!!!". Using one or two trigger words rarely causes deliverability problems on its own, but combining several significantly increases spam score. Subject lines in ALL CAPS are also heavily penalised.

---

**Q: What are email power words and how do they boost open rates?**

Power words are terms that trigger psychological responses — curiosity, urgency, social proof, exclusivity, or personal relevance — and increase the likelihood someone opens the email. Categories include: urgency words (today, now, expires, last chance), curiosity words (secret, revealed, what nobody tells you, the truth about), exclusivity words (members only, private, invite-only, exclusive), value words (save, free, results, profit), and personalisation (you, your, [first name]). Power words work because they activate emotional decision-making rather than analytical evaluation. Overusing them causes reader fatigue — use one or two per subject line.

**Q: How do I improve email open rates?**

Use 40–60 characters with your core hook in the first 35. Include one or two power words from the curiosity or urgency category. Remove any spam triggers. Add a specific number where possible ("5 mistakes" outperforms "common mistakes"). Keep punctuation to one mark — one question mark or one exclamation point, not both, and never three exclamation points. Limit emojis to one, and only where it adds meaning not decoration. Test with the A/B compare mode before sending. Also review your preview text (preheader) — it appears beside the subject line in most clients and is a second chance to earn the open.

**Q: What is A/B testing for email subject lines?**

A/B testing (split testing) sends two versions of an email with different subject lines to random equal segments of your list, then measures which gets a higher open rate. Most email platforms (AWeber, Mailchimp, ConvertKit) support this natively. The winning subject line is then sent to the remainder of the list. Use this tool's A/B compare mode to pre-screen candidates before running a full send — it lets you eliminate obvious losers without wasting a send cycle on them.

**Q: Does capitalisation affect email open rates?**

Yes. Title Case Subject Lines perform about the same as Sentence case subject lines in most studies. ALL CAPS subject lines perform significantly worse — they read as shouting and trigger spam filters. Using all lowercase (like this) can feel conversational and personal, which works for newsletters and informal brands but looks out of place for B2B or transactional email. Match capitalisation to your brand voice. Avoid CAPITALISING RANDOM WORDS for emphasis — it reads as spam.
