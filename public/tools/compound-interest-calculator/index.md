## What Is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest (which only earns on the original amount), compound interest earns interest on interest — which is why long investment timelines produce dramatically larger returns. Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest the eighth wonder of the world: those who understand it earn it, those who don't pay it.

## The Compound Interest Formula

The formula is: **A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)**

Where A is the final amount, P is the principal (starting amount), r is the annual interest rate as a decimal, n is the number of compounding periods per year, and t is the time in years. This calculator handles annual and monthly compounding and adds optional regular contributions to the calculation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What is compound interest and how does it work?**

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest, which only earns on the original deposit, compound interest earns interest on interest — meaning your balance grows faster over time. The longer you leave money invested, the more dramatic the compounding effect becomes.

**Q: What is the Rule of 72?**

The Rule of 72 is a shortcut to estimate how long it takes an investment to double. Divide 72 by the annual interest rate to get the approximate number of years. For example, at 8% per year, 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years to double. At 6%, it takes approximately 12 years. The rule works because the natural log of 2 (about 0.693) divided into 1 equals roughly 1/72 at typical interest rates.

**Q: Does daily compounding make a big difference compared to annual compounding?**

Less than most people expect. On £10,000 at 5% for 10 years, annual compounding produces £16,288 while daily compounding produces £16,487 — a difference of just £199. The compounding frequency matters much less than the interest rate and time horizon. Maximising your rate and investing early has far more impact than choosing daily vs annual compounding.

**Q: How do monthly contributions affect compound interest?**

Monthly contributions dramatically accelerate wealth growth because each contribution immediately begins compounding. £200 per month at 7% for 20 years produces approximately £104,000 — compared to a lump sum of £4,800 (the same total contributions with no growth). Each regular contribution adds a new compounding base, which is why dollar-cost averaging into investments over time outperforms waiting to invest a lump sum.

**Q: Is compound interest the same as APY?**

APY (Annual Percentage Yield) is the effective annual rate that accounts for compounding. APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the stated rate before compounding. If a savings account advertises 4.88% APR compounded monthly, the APY is approximately 5%. When comparing investment accounts or loans, always compare APY figures, not APR, to see the true cost or return.
